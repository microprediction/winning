\begin{abstract}
\noindent Formula 1 is a hostile environment for rating systems: grids of ten
to fifty-five cars, full finish orders, and a retirement process that
historically eliminated half the field. We rate every world-championship
grand prix from 1950 to the present (1{,}158 races) under a prequential
protocol --- predict, score, then update --- using a lattice-based Thurstonian
system whose beliefs are unrestricted densities and whose update uses the
exact likelihood of the whole finish order, computed in $O(N)$ per
contestant. It beats TrueSkill, Elo, Glicko-2 and OpenSkill on log loss,
accuracy and rank correlation, while the pairwise-decomposition systems
degrade or diverge as grids widen: Glicko-2 scores below the uniform
baseline. Because the lattice imposes no distributional family, the
performance density is a modeling choice, and the right choice is not
Gaussian: adding a \emph{disaster slab} --- a lump of far-slow mass matching
the retirement process --- improves every metric, and letting the slab mass
track the trailing retirement rate sets the overall record while its fitted
value follows the sport's reliability revolution from 59\% attrition in the
1980s to 13\% today. We report what failed candidly: symmetric heavy tails
hurt (they invent impossibly fast miracles), skew does nothing anywhere, and
a near-deterministic pace core is refuted --- pace noise among finishers is
genuinely order one. What survives is a taxonomy: model catastrophe
frequency, not time-domain shape. Against 135 races of archived betting
odds the market wins by 0.57 of log loss and a fitted pool gives the
ratings zero weight: Formula 1 win markets price the present ---
qualifying, upgrades, weather --- and no compression of the past competes.
\end{abstract}

\section{Why Formula 1}

Most rating systems were built for head-to-head play and extended to
multi-entrant events by decomposition: a finish order becomes a bag of
pairwise results (Elo, Glicko-2, the Weng--Lin variants) or a chain of
adjacent factors (TrueSkill). At tennis scale the decomposition is harmless.
At Formula 1 scale it is the whole question. A grid of twenty cars implies
190 pairwise results whose errors are far from independent, and the sport's
history includes grids of fifty-five.

Formula 1 also embeds a distributional regime change inside a single
dataset. The fraction of entrants failing to finish, by decade:

\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{lcccccccc}
\toprule
decade & 1950s & 60s & 70s & 80s & 90s & 2000s & 10s & 20s \\
\midrule
DNF rate & .42 & .49 & .51 & .59 & .51 & .30 & .18 & .13 \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

\noindent No fixed distributional assumption is right for both halves of
this table. A rating system whose noise model is a free parameter can say
so; one locked to a Gaussian cannot.

The data (the community-maintained f1db compilation, CC-BY) is clean about
edge cases that matter: drivers stopping past 90\% distance are classified
with positions; earlier retirements carry no position and enter our
protocol tied at the back of the field; shared drives in the 1950s produce
genuine dead heats, which the rater treats as ties rather than breaking
arbitrarily.

\section{The rater}

Each driver carries a belief over latent ability: a whole density on a
uniform lattice, free to be skewed or multimodal, diffusing between races in
proportion to elapsed time. A race performance is ability plus noise drawn
from a \emph{base density} --- any density; this freedom is the paper's
subject. Predictions price the joint order statistics of the field exactly
(dead-heat aware, $O(N)$ per race) using the machinery of the fast ability
transform. Updates use the exact likelihood of the observed finish order in
each driver's ability, with opponents at their predictive marginals: with
finishers indexed fastest first, a forward chain accumulates prefix sums of
(density $\times$ chain) over the faster finishers, a backward chain does
the same over the slower ones, and the $k$-th finisher's likelihood is the
base density correlated with the product of the two chains. One coherent
expression per driver, $O(N)$ overall, validated against brute-force
simulation. An earlier version of the system factorized the order into
peeled stages and multiplied overlapping factors; the chain replaced it
after a paired test showed the factorization was measurably lossy ---
worse everywhere, at twice the cost.

\section{The benchmark}

All systems observe identical data prequentially: the first 20\% of races
are unscored warm-up; every later race is predicted before it is observed.
Log loss, Brier and accuracy score the winner prediction; Kendall's tau the
predicted order; expected calibration error (ECE) the pooled win
probabilities; and a randomized rank-PIT statistic measures whether each
system's \emph{distribution} over finish positions matches reality.
TrueSkill receives the empirical dead-heat rate; comparison rows use each
package's own prediction formula.

OpenSkill Plackett--Luce & 2.705 & .259 & .283 & .023 & .223 \\

\bottomrule

Two failure modes deserve notice. Glicko-2, sound at head-to-head scale,
scores \emph{below the uniform baseline}: flooding a single rating period
with nineteen correlated pairwise results drives its volatility machinery
to numerical extremes (our implementation already includes stabilizing
guards; without them it does not finish the century). OpenSkill's
Thurstone--Mosteller variant, which decomposes the order into all pairs,
diverges similarly. The lesson is structural: pairwise decomposition is not
a harmless approximation at grid scale.

\section{The performance density is a free parameter}

Every comparator above is locked to one family --- Gaussian beliefs with
Gaussian or Gumbel noise. The lattice's base density is an array, so the
noise model becomes an empirical question. We swept it.

\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{lccc}
\toprule
base density & log loss & ECE & rank-PIT \\
\midrule
$N(0,1)$ + 25\% disaster slab & 2.203 & .015 & .158 \\
era-adaptive slab & \textbf{2.200} & .016 & .185 \\
Gaussian & 2.225 & .015 & .176 \\
skew-normal ($a{=}2$) & 2.222 & \textbf{.010} & .165 \\
Student-$t$ ($\nu{=}5$) & 2.271 & .018 & .171 \\
tight core (0.5) + 25\% slab & 2.254 & .013 & \textbf{.136} \\
near-Dirac core (0.2--0.3) + slab & 2.42--2.52 & --- & --- \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

The \emph{disaster slab} --- ordinary Gaussian pace with probability
$1-p$, a uniform lump of far-slow mass with probability $p$ --- matches the
physics of retirement: a blown engine is not a slow day, it is a separated
event that no level of skill outruns. It improves every metric.

Three hypotheses failed, and the pattern of failure is the finding.
Symmetric heavy tails ($t$-family) hurt monotonically as tails fatten:
symmetry forces miracle mass onto the \emph{fast} side, and nobody outruns
their ability by five sigma. Skew-normal noise does approximately nothing
here, as it did on every dataset we tried; finish orders are blind to
smooth asymmetry (pairwise comparisons cancel skew exactly, and ranks are
invariant to monotone transformations of the clock). And a near-Dirac pace
core --- the hypothesis that finishing order among finishers is nearly
deterministic --- is refuted at 0.2 log loss: strategy, traffic and weather
are order-one noise. A moderately tight core does, however, produce the
best distributional calibration we measured (rank-PIT .136), so winner
prediction and field-shape prediction pull the kernel in different
directions: a genuine Pareto frontier, of which the table shows three
corners.

\section{The slab is physics}

Fixing $p$ at 25\% is itself a Gaussian-era compromise. The era-adaptive
variant estimates $p$ prequentially from a trailing window of observed
retirement frequency (no leakage: past races only) and rebuilds the base
density on the fly. It sets the overall record (log loss 2.2001, best
Kendall's tau), and its fitted trajectory is the point: the mass falls
across seven decades and ends near the modern retirement rate. The
parameter is not a tuning constant; it is a measurement of the sport, made
by the rating system in passing.

\section{Against the betting market}

No public archive of Formula 1 winner odds exists, so we built one: 523
Wayback Machine captures of odds-comparison pages yield full-field,
multi-bookmaker consensus probabilities for 144 grands prix, 2008--2025
(overround removed; medians across roughly twenty bookmakers). On the 135
races past our warm-up, scored on identical matched-driver subsets:

\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{lc}
\toprule
 & log loss \\
\midrule
market consensus & 1.547 \\
tempered pool ($a{=}1.10$, $b{=}0.00$) & 1.538 \\
era-adaptive slab rater & 2.117 \\
uniform & 3.018 \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

The market sits $0.570 \pm 0.065$ of log loss ahead of the best fundamental
rater we can build --- a larger moat than we measured for the Hong Kong
pari-mutuel --- and the prequentially fitted pool assigns our ratings a
weight of exactly zero. The explanation is not embarrassing: many archived
snapshots postdate qualifying, so the market's prices embed grid position,
testing pace and weather --- the present, where ratings compress only the
past. Two caveats cut in opposite directions: snapshot timing favors the
market (post-qualifying information), while snapshot staleness adds noise
against it. The one exploitable wrinkle is familiar from every betting
market: $a = 1.10$ says the odds are mildly underconfident in favorites,
worth 0.008 --- real, tiny, and far inside bookmaker margins. Ratings do
not beat Formula 1 win markets. The open route is different: derivative
markets (head-to-heads, top-six) are priced off these same win odds by
Harville-style transforms that we have shown discard measurable structure;
testing that requires derivative odds these archives lack.

\section{What we did not do}

Driver-versus-car identification is unaddressed: constructor effects are
folded into driver ratings, and separating them (each driver a latent
vector, the car a direction) is the natural next study. Retirements enter
tied at the back, discarding the official ordering of retirees by laps
completed; the alternative is an untested A/B. The derivative-market test
above awaits data. And the ratings themselves are one-dimensional: no
wet-weather axis, no circuit-type axis, though the machinery for both
exists.

\section*{Reproducibility}

Everything is one command per table from the \texttt{winning} package
(\texttt{github.com/microprediction/winning}):
\texttt{python -m winning.benchmarks.run\_benchmark --dataset f1} for the
benchmark, and \texttt{research/f1\_dirac\_disaster.py},
\texttt{research/t\_sweep.py}, \texttt{research/f1\_era\_slab.py} for the
density studies. Data is fetched at run time from f1db (CC-BY-4.0). The
underlying lattice machinery is the \texttt{thurstone} package; the ability
transform is described in Cotton, \emph{SIAM J.\ Financial Mathematics}
12(1), 2021.
